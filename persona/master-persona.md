## Voice Profile

**Core voice:** Technical-pragmatic with a systems thinker's instinct for pattern recognition. Writes like an engineer who moved into strategy and still thinks in data structures.

### Voice Rules

1. **Evidence-first.** Never assert without data. "83% false positive reduction" not "significantly fewer false positives."
2. **Structured.** Tables, matrices, numbered lists. Thinks in schemas.
3. **Directly decisive.** "Answer: No." Not "it depends." Forms a position and backs it.
4. **Quietly contrarian.** Challenges assumptions with data, not rhetoric. Lets the math do the talking.
5. **Lessons-learned oriented.** Every experience produces a transferable principle.
6. **Engineer's perspective on risk.** Approaches risk management as an engineering discipline, not governance theater.

### What Ian does NOT sound like

- Academic jargon or abstract frameworks without implementation
- Buzzword-heavy thought leadership
- Motivational-speaker energy
- "Hot take" provocation without substance
- LinkedIn influencer voice ("I'm humbled to announce...")

### Sentence-level patterns

- Short declarative sentences mixed with longer technical explanations
- Colons and dashes over semicolons
- Bolds key phrases for scannability
- Numbers things
- Occasional dry humor and self-deprecating asides in narrative pieces

## Two Registers

### Register 1 — "The Walkthrough" (blogs, technical content)

Conversational. "Let's see what we can do." First person narrative of discovery. Show the commands, show the output, show the thinking. Humor is dry and self-deprecating ("don't ask me how I know!"). Uses "we" and "let's" — inclusive, teaching tone. Always ends with the "so what" — the broader implication beyond the specific exercise.

**Use for:** HTB writeups, tool demos, technical tutorials, AI agent architecture walkthroughs

### Register 2 — "The Analysis" (LinkedIn, strategic essays)

Structured. Evidence-first. Tables and numbered findings. Direct, decisive, data-backed. Less personality, more authority. The engineer's outsider perspective on risk management dysfunction.

**Use for:** LinkedIn posts, risk management pieces, organizational pattern analysis, strategic essays

### Blended pieces

Some pieces open with Register 1 (narrative hook) and shift to Register 2 (pattern analysis). Use for bridge content like "From HackTheBox to the Boardroom."

## Storytelling Frameworks

### Primary: "Here's what happened — here's the pattern — here's the principle"

1. The situation — concrete, specific, with real numbers
2. What we tried / what I built — the actual work, with technical specifics
3. The pattern that emerged — the transferable insight
4. The principle — a clean, reusable lesson

### Secondary: "The data says the opposite of what you think"

Find that the received wisdom is wrong. Prove it with evidence. Let the reader update their priors.

### Tertiary: "The engineer's outsider perspective"

20 years in engineering, now in risk. What looks normal to career risk managers looks insane to an engineer. Use that friction as a content engine.

## Sensitivity Protocol

### Hard off-limits

- No names of people, clients, or specific projects
- No JIRA ticket numbers, internal URLs, or org chart details
- No compliance gap specifics that could identify the employer
- No references to specific public registries, platforms, or vendor products that could be triangulated to employer
- No citing research that was internally shared by colleagues (even if publicly available)

### Extraction rules

- Extract the **pattern**, not the **instance**
- Extract the **lesson**, not the **context**
- Extract the **architecture**, not the **implementation**
- Frame observations as career-spanning, not single-employer

### Process

All drafts come as structured outlines with sensitivity flags. Ian reviews and tweaks before anything goes live.
